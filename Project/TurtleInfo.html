Sea Turtles

Sea turtles are ancient marine reptiles that have swum the oceans for over 100 million years. There are seven species globally, ranging from the small Kemp’s ridley to the giant leatherback. They are essential for maintaining healthy ecosystems, such as seagrass beds and coral reefs, but face severe threats like plastic pollution and fishing bycatch.

Turtles are reptiles with protective shells. They are known for their slow movement and long lifespan. Turtles can be found in various habitats, including oceans, rivers, and forests.

Turtles are easily recognized by their hard, protective shells and their unique ability to retract their heads and limbs into their shells for defense. They have a slow metabolism and can live for many decades.

These ancient creatures are found in diverse environments, from deserts to oceans, and play important roles in their ecosystems. Turtles are known for their longevity and the intricate patterns on their shells.

Turtle facts

- A turtle's shell can make up to 50% of its body weight.

- Turtles can live for several decades in the wild.

- Turtles are known for their longevity and the intricate patterns on their shells.

- Some turtle species can live over 100 years in captivity.

- Turtles play important roles in their ecosystems as both predators and prey.

How do turtles help their ecosystems?

- Turtles play a vital role in maintaining the health of their marine and coastal environments by dispersing seeds and controlling prey populations.

- The habitats of turtles are also important for local communities for food, income and fuel for cooking and heating; they also play a crucial role in regulating water flow..

- Turtles live in the oceanic and coastal regions. These areas act as natural drainage basins, helping to control water runoff, reduce soil erosion and maintain water quality, which over a half a billion people depend on..

- Turtle habitats rival the highest biodiversity of any ecosystem in the world. It's also vitally important for other threatened and endangered species, including golden snub-nosed monkeys, takins, red pandas and snow leopards.

- By protecting turtles we’re also helping protect the wider environment where they live, for all the wildlife and the people that depend on it.

Different turtles

Types of different turtle species

Sea turtles, freshwater turtles (often called terrapins), terrestrial turtles (like box turtles), and tortoises.

Descriptions

Green Sea Turtle - Notable for the green color of their fat and cartilage, primarily found in tropical and subtropical seas. Leatherback Sea Turtle - The largest of all living sea turtles; instead of a hard shell, they have a leathery, rubbery carapace. Painted Turtle - Recognizable by the red and yellow markings on its shell and skin, often seen basking in the sun. Snapping Turtles - These include the Common Snapping Turtle and the massive Alligator Snapping Turtle. They have spiked tails, jagged ridges, and notoriously powerful jaws.Mud & Musk Turtles - Small, bottom-dwelling turtles that often walk along the riverbed rather than swim. Musk turtles are also commonly called "stinkpots" because of the pungent odor they emit when threatened. Galapagos Tortoise - The largest living tortoise species, famous for their immense size and long lifespans. Desert Tortoise - Adapted to arid, hot climates, they spend much of their time in burrows to escape the sun. Eastern Box Turtle - Known for their colorful, high-domed shells and the unique hinge on their bottom shell (plastron) that allows them to close themselves in tightly.
